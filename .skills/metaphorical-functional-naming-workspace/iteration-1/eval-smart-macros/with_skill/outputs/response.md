# Naming: Adaptive Action Sequences ("Smart Macros")

## Reasoning

### Core function

This feature records a sequence of user actions and replays them, but with awareness of context changes. A traditional macro is a rigid recording - it plays back exactly what was captured. This feature is different because it *adapts*. It understands the structure of what it recorded, not just the literal steps, so it can adjust when the environment shifts.

The fundamental mechanism here is not just "recording and playback." It is closer to *learning a routine and performing it in new conditions*. Think of the difference between a player piano roll (fixed) and a musician who learned a piece and can transpose it to a different key on the fly. The core action is: **capture intent, then re-execute that intent faithfully even when the terrain has changed.**

### Metaphor exploration

Several metaphor domains are worth exploring:

- **Navigation/pathfinding** - Something that knows a route but can reroute when the road changes. A guide who adapts to terrain.
- **Music/performance** - A musician who plays from memory but adjusts to the room, the tempo, the ensemble. Not a recording, but a performer.
- **Craft/trades** - A practiced hand that knows the motions but adjusts to the grain of the wood. Muscle memory that responds to material.
- **Nature/biology** - Instinct or reflex - learned behavior that fires in context. Migration patterns that adjust to weather. Water that follows the same downhill intent but takes the path the landscape provides.
- **Theater/choreography** - A choreographed routine that dancers adapt to the stage they are on.
- **Weaving/textiles** - A pattern that repeats but adjusts to the width of the loom or the weight of the thread.

---

## Evaluation of "Reel"

**What works:** "Reel" is short, concrete, and real. It evokes film reels and fishing reels - both involve winding, storing, and playing back a sequence. It has a nice kinetic quality. As an internal name it is easy to say, easy to type, and memorable.

**What doesn't work:** The strongest association is with film reels or Instagram Reels - both of which imply *exact playback* of a fixed recording. A reel plays the same thing every time. That is precisely the limitation this feature transcends. The name lands on the "macro" half of the concept (record and replay) but misses the "smart" half (adapts to context). It also carries heavy social-media connotations now thanks to Instagram/TikTok Reels, which may cause confusion or feel dated quickly.

**Verdict:** Reel is a serviceable internal name, but it undersells the adaptive intelligence that makes this feature distinctive. A stronger name would hint at the contextual flexibility, not just the sequence capture.

## Candidates

### 1. Groove

A groove is a carved track that guides movement - like a groove in a record or a groove in machinery. But "getting into a groove" also means finding a practiced rhythm that flows naturally and adjusts fluidly. It implies learned, repeatable motion that is not rigid but *fluid*. A groove guides without constraining.

### 2. Cadence

A cadence is a rhythmic pattern - in speech, in music, in running. It is repeatable and structured, but it naturally adapts to pace, context, and conditions. A runner's cadence adjusts to hills. A speaker's cadence adjusts to the room. It captures "a practiced sequence that responds to its environment."

### 3. Reflex

A reflex is a learned response that fires automatically in context. It is fast, reliable, and adaptive - your reflexes work whether you are standing, sitting, or off-balance. It implies something that was trained once and now executes intelligently without rigid scripting. The adaptation is built in.

### 4. Trace

A trace is a path left behind - a trail that can be followed again. But following a trace is not the same as following exact coordinates; you read the trace and navigate the current terrain. It implies "recorded intent" rather than "recorded steps." Lightweight and understated.

### 5. Gait

A gait is a pattern of movement - walking, trotting, galloping. It is consistent and recognizable, but it adapts automatically to the surface, the slope, the speed. Your gait on sand is different from your gait on pavement, but it is still *your gait*. This captures the idea of a repeatable action pattern that naturally adjusts to context.

### 6. Relay

A relay passes something forward through stages, with each stage picking up and carrying on. It implies a sequence of handoffs that completes a larger task. A relay adapts because each leg of the race is run by a different runner on different terrain, but the baton gets where it needs to go.

### 7. Plait

A plait (braid) is a repeating pattern of interwoven strands. Each repetition of the pattern adjusts to the tension and material. It implies structured repetition with built-in flexibility. Less common as a word, which gives it some distinctiveness, though it may be less immediately legible to all audiences.

---

## Strongest candidates

### Groove

The best overall fit. It nails both halves of the concept: "a groove" is a repeatable track (the macro/sequence aspect), and "in a groove" is practiced, adaptive flow (the smart/contextual aspect). It is one syllable, universally understood, has positive connotations (smooth, practiced, effortless), and does not carry baggage from competing products. It would work well in UI ("Create a Groove," "Run this Groove," "Your Grooves") and in conversation ("I set up a groove for onboarding new clients"). Calm, durable, brandable.

### Reflex

The sharpest at communicating the adaptive intelligence. A reflex is not a script - it is a trained response that fires correctly regardless of context. This name foregrounds the "smart" aspect: these are not dumb recordings, they are learned behaviors. It works well in UI ("Add a Reflex," "This Reflex adapts to...") and implies speed and reliability. Slightly more technical in feel than Groove, but very clear.

### Cadence

The most elegant at capturing "a repeating pattern that naturally adjusts." Cadence implies discipline and rhythm without rigidity. It works especially well if the feature involves sequences that repeat on a schedule or have a temporal component. It is a bit longer (two syllables) and a bit more abstract than Groove, but it is distinctive and has no competing product associations.
