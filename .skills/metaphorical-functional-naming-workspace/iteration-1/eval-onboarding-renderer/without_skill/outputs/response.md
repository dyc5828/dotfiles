# Naming: Cross-Platform Onboarding Renderer

## The Problem

A system that takes a single onboarding flow definition and generates platform-specific implementations for web, iOS, Android, and email. Write once, render everywhere for onboarding experiences.

---

## Name Suggestions

### 1. Onboard

**Why:** Simple, verb-forward, immediately communicable. "We defined the flow in Onboard" works in conversation. The name is the action. It positions the tool as the canonical way onboarding gets done, not just a renderer or compiler. Short enough to become a CLI command, a library import, or a product name.

**Tradeoffs:** Generic. Could collide with existing products. Doesn't hint at the cross-platform magic.

---

### 2. Threshold

**Why:** A threshold is the point of entry - the doorway you cross when entering a new space. Onboarding is exactly that: guiding someone across the threshold into your product. The metaphor also captures the idea of a single defined boundary that exists regardless of which door (platform) you walk through. One threshold, many doors.

**Tradeoffs:** Poetic but not immediately self-describing. Requires a sentence of explanation the first time someone hears it. That said, it sticks once explained.

---

### 3. Bloom

**Why:** A bloom is a single seed producing many flowers. One definition, many platform outputs. It also carries the connotation of a first experience - a user "blooming" into your product. Works well as a verb: "Bloom that flow to iOS." Feels alive and growth-oriented, which matches the optimistic energy of good onboarding.

**Tradeoffs:** Soft/organic tone may not fit every engineering culture. No obvious connection to rendering or compilation.

---

### 4. Prism

**Why:** White light enters a prism and splits into a spectrum. One input, many outputs - each adapted to its medium but originating from the same source. This maps precisely to the system's architecture: a single flow definition refracts into web, iOS, Android, and email. The metaphor is technically accurate and visually memorable.

**Tradeoffs:** Already used by several developer tools (Stoplight Prism, etc.). The "splitting" metaphor might imply divergence rather than consistency, though a prism's outputs are all fundamentally the same light.

---

### 5. Loom

**Why:** A loom takes a single pattern (the warp and weft design) and weaves it into fabric. One pattern, consistent output across the material. "Loom" suggests craftsmanship - that the onboarding experience is deliberately woven, not randomly generated. It also subtly references threads (as in execution threads across platforms). Works naturally: "Define the flow in Loom, it handles the rest."

**Tradeoffs:** Conflicts with the popular screen recording tool Loom. In internal/framework contexts this may not matter, but for a public product it would be a problem.

---

### 6. Forge

**Why:** A forge takes raw material and shapes it into finished forms. One mold, many castings. The name conveys transformation and production - you define the shape once, and the forge stamps out platform-specific implementations. Strong, industrial, implies reliability. "Forge the onboarding flow" reads naturally as both creation and compilation.

**Tradeoffs:** Very common in tech naming (GitHub, SourceForge, etc.). Industrial tone may feel heavy for something about user experience.

---

### 7. Runway

**Why:** A runway is where things launch. Every plane (platform) uses the same runway to take off. The onboarding flow is the runway - the shared starting surface from which each platform-specific experience launches. Also carries fashion connotations (a runway show presents one collection across many looks), which maps to the idea of a single design rendered in different platform-native styles.

**Tradeoffs:** Conflicts with Runway ML. The aviation metaphor is more about launching than about the cross-platform rendering aspect.

---

### 8. Facet

**Why:** A facet is one face of a cut gem - same stone, different angles. Each platform rendering is a facet of the same onboarding definition. The name captures both unity (one gem) and multiplicity (many faces) without implying that any platform is primary. "The iOS facet" and "the web facet" feel natural.

**Tradeoffs:** Slightly abstract. Doesn't immediately say "onboarding" or "rendering." Works better as a framework name than a product name.

---

## Top Recommendation

**Threshold** for a public-facing product or named framework. The metaphor is precise (point of entry, one boundary regardless of door), memorable, and carries real weight. It tells a story that makes the system's purpose click immediately.

**Prism** as the runner-up if you want something that foregrounds the technical architecture (one input, many outputs) over the onboarding metaphor.

**Bloom** if the team leans toward warmth and wants the name to reflect the user's experience rather than the system's mechanics.
