# Structural Metaphor Naming

Generate names by finding the deeper mechanism or metaphor underneath what something does, then surfacing a short, concrete, real-world word that implies that mechanism.

## Theoretical Foundation

This naming approach draws from established frameworks across cognitive linguistics, branding theory, and design.

**Structural metaphor** (Lakoff & Johnson, *Metaphors We Live By*, 1980) describes how people systematically understand one concept in terms of another. TIME IS MONEY. ARGUMENT IS WAR. This naming style applies the same principle: understand a product's mechanism in terms of a physical-world system, then use the physical-world word as the name.

**Relational metaphor** (Gentner, Structure-Mapping Theory, 1983) distinguishes between mapping surface attributes and mapping relational structure. "Her eyes are diamonds" maps an attribute (sparkle). "The atom is a solar system" maps a mechanism (objects orbiting a center). This naming style is strictly relational - it maps how the thing *works*, not how it looks or what it reminds you of.

**In branding taxonomy**, this falls under what the Igor naming framework calls "evocative names" and what ICU Agency more precisely calls "analogy metaphor" - names based on structural or functional similarity between the name's referent and the product. In trademark law, these are "suggestive marks" - names that require an imaginative leap but illuminate the product once the connection lands.

**What distinguishes this style** from generic metaphorical naming:
- The source is always a concrete physical object or process, not an abstraction
- The mapping is mechanistic - it maps how the thing works, not a mood or association
- The metaphor must be immediate - if it needs a sentence of explanation, it's too abstract
- The word must have a physical anchor - you should be able to picture the object or scene

## Process

Before generating any names, do a brief reasoning step. This keeps names grounded in what the thing actually does rather than what it sounds like on the surface.

1. **Identify the core function.** What does this thing fundamentally do? Not the feature list - the underlying action, role, or mechanism. A transcription tool doesn't just "transcribe" - it captures and preserves a flow of information. A deployment pipeline doesn't just "deploy" - it forges raw materials into a finished artifact.

2. **Find the metaphor underneath.** Look for a physical object, natural process, trade, or system that operates the same way. Cast a wide net across domains: navigation, storage, measurement, construction, transformation, infrastructure, natural processes, trades, environments. Don't collapse onto one metaphor family unless the product clearly calls for it. Keep this section brief - identify the domains, don't name candidates yet.

3. **Generate 5-8 candidate names.** Each should be a real English word - short, concrete, and implying the mechanism without describing the category literally. Do not repeat names that appeared in the reasoning step. The candidate list is the single, definitive list of names.

4. **Evaluate the strongest candidates.** Briefly explain why 2-3 of the best names fit. Consider: does the name imply the right action? Is it legible and brandable? Does it feel calm and durable rather than trendy or whimsical?

5. **If the user already has a candidate name**, evaluate it honestly against these criteria before or alongside your suggestions. Say what works about it, what doesn't, and whether it holds up.

## Naming Style

**Prefer:**
- Short, often one word
- Real words drawn from the physical world
- Names that imply the underlying action, mechanism, role, or substrate
- Calm, durable, legible, brandable
- **Tactile, sensory, kinetic words** - names you can almost physically feel. Tremor, Groove, Prism, Stencil, Anvil. These activate a sense of texture, motion, or material. The best names feel like you could pick them up.
- **Words with a physical anchor** - every name should trigger a concrete visual or physical association. This can be an object (Spool, Sieve, Anvil) or an action, as long as the action has a vivid physical scene behind it (Forge works because you see fire, metal, hammering). What doesn't work are action words that feel abstract or disembodied - Cull, Parse, Resolve. If you can't picture something physical happening or a thing you could hold, the word lacks the tangibility that makes a good name.
- **Immediate metaphors** - the connection between the name and the thing should click without explanation. If someone needs a sentence to understand why the name fits, it's probably too abstract. Tremor for an early-warning system clicks instantly. Threshold for an onboarding tool does not.

**Avoid:**
- Invented brand names or portmanteaus
- Generic labels: Recorder, Notes, Transcript, Assistant, AI, Copilot, Platform, Hub, Cloud, Sync
- Whimsical, overly abstract, or fantasy-flavored names
- Startup-name-generator outputs
- Describing the category literally
- **Abstract or conceptual names** - words like Threshold, Bloom, Motif, Paradigm. These live in the head, not the hands. They require interpretation rather than triggering instant recognition. A name should feel concrete even if it is metaphorical.
- **Literary or poetic names** that sound good in a sentence but don't map to a physical thing you can picture
- **Words with competing primary meanings** - if the most common association of a word points somewhere other than the intended metaphor, the name fails. "Die" as a manufacturing stamping tool is a valid metaphor, but most people think of death or dice first. The intended meaning has to be the one people reach for naturally, not a specialized or secondary definition.

## Example Names in This Style

These illustrate the range - notice how they span many different metaphor domains:

- **Spool** - for something that winds up and feeds out a continuous thread of content
- **Forge** - for something that takes raw inputs and hammers them into a finished form
- **Beacon** - for something that broadcasts a signal or makes something findable
- **Ledger** - for something that maintains an authoritative record of transactions or state
- **Harbor** - for something that provides safe arrival, docking, and departure
- **Prism** - for something that takes a single input and refracts it into multiple distinct outputs
- **Compass** - for something that helps orient and find direction
- **Pulse** - for something that monitors a heartbeat or ongoing health signal
- **Quarry** - for something that extracts raw material from a large mass
- **Lantern** - for something that illuminates a specific area in darkness
- **Anvil** - for something that provides the hard surface against which things get shaped
- **Atlas** - for something that maps a large territory into a navigable reference

These are examples of the style, not a fixed vocabulary. Draw from whatever domain fits the thing being named.
