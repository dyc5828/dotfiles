# Naming Evaluation: Smart Adaptive Macros

## The Feature

A system that lets users save and replay sequences of actions, like macros but with the ability to adapt to context changes.

## Evaluation of "Reel"

**Reel** is a decent starting point but has some notable issues.

**What works:**
- Short, one syllable, easy to say
- Evokes the idea of a sequence playing back, like a film reel
- Has a natural verb form: "reel it back," "run that reel"

**What doesn't work:**
- Strong existing association with Instagram/TikTok Reels, which are short video clips. This creates confusion rather than clarity. Users will think "short video" before they think "replayable action sequence."
- A film reel is a passive playback medium. It doesn't suggest adaptability or intelligence, which is the core differentiator of this feature.
- "Reel" as a verb also means to stagger or be disoriented, which is not a great secondary association.
- Doesn't hint at the "smart" part at all. It sounds like a dumb replay.

**Verdict:** Reel captures the "sequence of actions" half but completely misses the "adapts to context" half. You can do better.

---

## Alternative Suggestions

### Tier 1: Strong Recommendations

**1. Groove**
A groove is a well-worn path, a pattern that flows naturally. Musicians "find a groove" - it's a repeatable pattern that still responds to the moment. This captures both the repeatable sequence and the adaptive, contextual feel. "Run that groove" or "I set up a groove for onboarding new clients" both sound natural. It implies something practiced and fluid rather than rigid.

**2. Reflex**
A reflex is a learned, automatic response that still adapts to stimulus. Your reflexes don't fire the exact same way every time - they respond to the specific situation. This directly communicates "automatic action that responds to context." "Set up a reflex" and "my deploy reflex" both work well. It also implies speed and reliability.

**3. Cadence**
A cadence is a rhythmic pattern that can vary while maintaining its essential structure. Runners adjust their cadence to terrain. Speakers adjust cadence to audience. It communicates "repeatable pattern with built-in flexibility." Works well as a noun: "Create a cadence," "Run that cadence." Slightly more formal tone, which might fit depending on your product's voice.

### Tier 2: Solid Options

**4. Relay**
A relay passes work forward, with each leg adapting to conditions. It implies a chain of actions with handoffs. "Set up a relay" is intuitive. The downside is it slightly over-emphasizes the handoff between steps rather than the adaptive intelligence.

**5. Motif**
A motif is a recurring theme that appears in different contexts with variations. In music, a motif gets reinterpreted across movements. This captures "same essential pattern, different execution based on context" very precisely. Slightly more abstract, which could be a strength or weakness depending on your audience.

**6. Trace**
A trace records a path that can be followed again. In computing, traces already imply recorded sequences of operations. "Trace and replay" is intuitive. The adaptive angle is weaker here, but the base concept is strong and familiar to technical users.

**7. Drift**
A drift is a pattern that naturally adjusts course. It implies movement with responsive correction. Interesting and distinctive, but might sound too passive or aimless for an automation feature.

### Tier 3: Worth Considering

**8. Pulse**
Rhythmic, recurring, alive. A pulse adapts - heart rate responds to activity. But it might suggest monitoring or health checks more than action sequences.

**9. Riff**
A musical riff is a repeatable phrase that musicians improvise on. Captures the "same base pattern, adapted in the moment" idea well. Very casual tone. Could feel too informal for some products.

**10. Circuit**
A circuit is a defined path that returns to start, but "circuit training" implies adapting intensity. Has a technical feel. Might sound too mechanical for the adaptive quality you want to highlight.

## Final Recommendation

**Go with Groove or Reflex.**

If your product voice is warm and approachable, **Groove** is the pick. It feels natural, implies mastery through repetition, and the adaptive quality is baked into the metaphor. "Create a Groove" and "Your Grooves" work well in UI.

If your product voice is more technical or precise, **Reflex** is sharper. It directly communicates "automatic response that adapts to stimulus," which is essentially your feature description in one word. "Set up a Reflex" and "Your Reflexes" both scan well.

Either one is a significant upgrade over Reel because they communicate both halves of the feature - the repeatable sequence AND the contextual intelligence - in a single word.
