# Naming: Smart Adaptive Macros Feature

## Evaluating "Reel"

**Reel** is a decent starting point but has some notable weaknesses:

**Strengths:**
- Short, punchy, one syllable - easy to say and type
- Evokes a sequence of frames playing back, which maps loosely to a sequence of actions replaying
- Familiar word, low cognitive load

**Weaknesses:**
- Heavy association with Instagram/TikTok Reels, which pulls users toward thinking "short video" rather than "automated action sequence"
- Doesn't convey the adaptive/smart aspect at all - a reel plays the same thing every time
- The film-reel metaphor implies rigid, linear playback - the opposite of "adapts to context changes"
- Could confuse users who think of fishing reels, which has no useful connotation here
- As a verb ("reel back," "reeling") it carries negative connotations

**Verdict:** Reel captures the "sequence of actions" idea but completely misses the defining feature - context-aware adaptation. You can do better.

---

## Alternative Name Suggestions

### Tier 1 - Strong Recommendations

**1. Groove**
A groove is a well-worn path - but in music, a groove adapts. Musicians don't play a groove identically every time; they respond to the room, the other players, the energy. This maps perfectly to "learned action sequences that adapt to context." It's short, positive, and verb-friendly: "Groove that workflow," "Run this Groove," "Create a new Groove."

**2. Reflex**
A biological reflex is a learned response that adapts based on conditions - your pupil dilates more in darker rooms, your hand pulls back faster from hotter surfaces. It conveys speed, automaticity, and context-sensitivity in a single word. "Set up a Reflex," "This Reflex handles form submissions." Also carries the connotation of something that becomes second nature.

**3. Motif**
In music and literature, a motif is a recurring pattern that transforms based on context - it appears in different keys, different scenes, different arrangements, but retains its essential identity. This is exactly what an adaptive macro does: same core sequence, different execution based on surroundings. "Create a Motif," "Run the onboarding Motif."

### Tier 2 - Solid Options

**4. Cadence**
A cadence is a rhythmic pattern that adapts to tempo, terrain, or conditions. Runners adjust their cadence; musicians shift theirs. It suggests a repeatable rhythm that flexes with circumstances. Slightly more syllables but professional-sounding.

**5. Ripple**
A ripple propagates outward and adapts to the surface it moves across - same initial action, different manifestation depending on context. Short, visual, pleasant. "Create a Ripple," "Run the deploy Ripple." The downside is it might suggest side effects or unintended consequences.

**6. Gist**
A gist captures the essence of something without being a rigid copy. It implies "the important parts" while leaving room for adaptation. Very short, developer-friendly due to GitHub Gists. However, that existing association could cause confusion.

**7. Trace**
A trace follows a path but adapts to the surface. In computing, a trace records execution flow. "Trace this workflow" is natural. The risk is it sounds more like debugging/observability than automation.

### Tier 3 - Worth Considering

**8. Weave**
Suggests threads of actions interlacing and adapting to each other. Rich metaphor but slightly abstract.

**9. Drift**
A controlled drift adapts to conditions while maintaining general direction. Cool-sounding but might imply lack of precision.

**10. Flux**
Implies continuous adaptation and change. Strong in developer contexts but might suggest instability.

---

## Recommendation

**Go with Groove or Reflex.**

- If the feature leans playful and user-facing, **Groove** is the pick. It's warm, intuitive, and the musical metaphor maps perfectly to "a pattern that adapts every time it plays."
- If the feature leans technical and power-user-facing, **Reflex** is the pick. It communicates speed, automaticity, and context-awareness with zero ambiguity.

Both are single words, easy to verbify, and - critically - they communicate the adaptive nature that makes this feature different from plain macros. That's the thing Reel misses entirely.
